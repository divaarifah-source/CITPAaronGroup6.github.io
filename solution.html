A Living Shoreline for a Living Culture

Our Solution: The Integrated Regenerative Coastal System (IRCS)

Working with nature to protect Saibai’s shores and restore its coastal resilience.

See the System

What is IRCS?

The Integrated Regenerative Coastal System is a hybrid, nature-based coastal defence built for Saibai Island. It pairs low-tech engineering such as brushwood bunds with ecological processes like mangrove regeneration and sediment trapping. Instead of creating a rigid wall, IRCS shapes a living shoreline that strengthens itself season after season.

As sediment settles behind the bunds and mangroves take root, wave energy drops and the shoreline rebuilds. The system grows more resilient over time because the community guides and maintains it.

Why IRCS?

Saibai’s existing seawalls offer short-term relief yet accelerate erosion, sever tidal exchange, and disconnect the community from Country. Our challenge analysis showed the shoreline losing land, livelihoods, and cultural access.

IRCS replaces brittle barriers with a restorative cycle that rebuilds land, revives mangroves, and empowers Saibai to steward its coastline. It is designed to adapt with tides, not against them.

Initial Design Options Assessed

Before progressing to IRCS we re-examined previously deployed defences to understand their limits and lessons.

Reinforced Seawalls

Upgrading concrete seawalls with higher aprons promised immediate flood defence yet amplified scour at the base and diverted erosion downstream.

Verdict: technically robust but financially intensive and culturally isolating, failing to restore tidal ecology.

Standalone Mangrove Replanting

Community planting campaigns revitalised select plots, but mortality remained high where hydrodynamics still stripped fine sediments.

Verdict: ecologically aligned yet fragile without engineered calm-water zones to shield seedlings.

Drainage & Channel Works

Re-cutting tidal channels improved flushing and reduced ponding, although benefits stayed localised and did not rebuild shoreline mass.

Verdict: useful supporting measure, but insufficient as a stand-alone coastal protection strategy.

How IRCS Works

A four-phase journey that restores tidal function, shelters sediment, and grows a living mangrove shield.

Phase 1 ‚Äî Foundational Preparation

Tidal channel reopening and sediment nourishment restore water exchange and raise the tidal flat, preparing stable ground for regeneration.

Phase 2 ‚Äî Structural Protection

Brushwood bunds are built with local labour. The porous fences calm waves so suspended sediment settles into a protected nursery zone.

Phase 3 ‚Äî Ecological Restoration

Mangrove propagule broadcasting and floating vegetation mats jump-start root establishment. Growing roots stitch the sediment and build a coastal forest.

Phase 4 ‚Äî Long-Term Sustainability

A Community Steering Group leads monitoring, repair, and blue carbon tracking. Their stewardship keeps the system adaptive and turns regeneration into income.

Meeting the Design Criteria

IRCS satisfies ecological harmony, community empowerment, and physical protection in one integrated system.

Ecological Harmony

Restores mangroves and wetlands, boosts biodiversity, and reinforces natural sediment retention processes.

Community Empowerment

Uses locally sourced materials, provides training, and hands daily stewardship to Saibai’s Community Steering Group.

Physical Protection

Dissipates wave energy, traps sediment, and prevents erosion more sustainably than rigid seawalls.

IRCS in Action

To validate IRCS’s sediment trapping, we built a Mini Wetland Sediment Trapping System using a clear bottle model. By slowing the inflow, suspended particles settled quickly, mirroring the calm water behind brushwood bunds.

Testing showed roughly 75% sediment retention, demonstrating that managed flow reduction enables the shoreline to rebuild.

Our prototype validated the core function of IRCS: slow the water, and the land returns.

Innovation & Sustainability

IRCS turns restoration into opportunity. Blue carbon credits reward mangrove expansion, while modular bund units allow other Torres Strait islands to adopt the system with minimal external input. By anchoring the work in community knowledge, the shoreline becomes both resilient habitat and sustainable livelihood.

By blending traditional knowledge with regenerative engineering, IRCS transforms Saibai’s coast from erosion to resilience.
