Why Saibai Is at Risk

Saibai Island sits within the Torres Strait, a low-lying mud island only kilometres from Papua New Guinea. Climate shifts are reshaping its coastline and livelihood.

Saibai‚Äôs foundations are layers of mangrove sediment, resting barely above sea level. Intensifying king tides, storm surges, and sea-level rise now inundate homes several times a year. Saltwater intrusion contaminates precious freshwater lenses, while chronic flooding accelerates shoreline loss and threatens food production and infrastructure.

These pressures compound on a community whose cultural identity is inseparable from Country. Elders describe how burial grounds and culturally significant trees are being swept away, eroding stories that have anchored Saibai life for generations.

This isn't just one problem. It's an environmental crisis, a cultural tragedy, and an economic nightmare‚Äîall happening simultaneously.

They've Tried Everything (And Nothing Worked)

üí∞ Concrete Seawalls: Millions Wasted

Governments spent a fortune building massive concrete barriers. They looked impressive. For a while, they seemed to work. Then cyclones cracked them. Waves undermined them. Worse, they bounced wave energy sideways, making erosion even worse in neighboring areas. Now they need constant repairs, draining budgets while the island keeps sinking. The walls failed. The community paid the price.

üå± Replanting Forests: Good Idea, Wrong Timing

Community members tried planting coastal forests by hand‚Äîbeautiful effort, heartbreaking results. Most seedlings died because the soil kept washing away before roots could take hold. You can't regrow a forest on an island that's actively eroding. It's like trying to plant a garden in a landslide. The community did everything right, but without fixing the erosion first, nature couldn't recover.

üö∞ Drainage Channels: Missing the Point

Engineers dug channels to help floodwater drain away. In some spots, it helped manage standing water. But it did nothing to stop the island from literally disappearing. The soil kept washing into the ocean. The shoreline kept retreating. It was treating symptoms while ignoring the disease.

But There IS a Way Forward

What if we stopped fighting nature and started working with it? What if we helped the island heal itself‚Äîthe same way it was formed thousands of years ago?

Our solution is beautifully simple: Use wooden structures to slow the waves and trap soil. Let the land build up naturally. Watch coastal forests regrow on their own. These living barriers protect better than any concrete wall‚Äîand they maintain themselves. No expensive contractors. No imported materials. Just smart design that puts the community in control and lets nature do what it does best.

This isn't just possible. It's already working in similar communities around the world.
