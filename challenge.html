The Context

Saibai Island & Climate Impact

Saibai sits on porous mangrove peat only centimetres above the tides.

Saibai's Bureau of Meteorology tide gauge shows sea level now sits 0.18 m higher than the 1992 baseline, with the January 2024 king tide peaking at 3.59 m. Each surge seeps through the peat flats, salting wells and cassava plots within hours.

During the February 2024 monsoon burst, eight days of storm tide closed the airstrip and the school road. Families moved vehicles onto the church mound while waves cut fresh ruts beside the seawall.

Elevation

88% of homes and clinics sit below 1.2 m above highest astronomical tide.

Storm tides

January-April 2024 recorded 28 inundation days, double the count logged a decade ago.

Coastal change

Ranger drone transects show shoreline retreat of 0.6 m per year across the village frontage.

Core Challenges

Pressures that threaten Saibai today

Each challenge links climate drivers with local conditions and needs a tailored, culturally led answer.

Challenge 1 · Foreshore Undercutting

The 1.5 km village frontage loses roughly 30 truckloads of mud each monsoon, letting salt water run beneath houses.

The Cause

North-west monsoon surges lift water 1.2 m above mean sea level and rebound off the ageing seawall.

The Impact

Thirty-two homes, the clinic ramp, and the cemetery road now flood on king tide weeks.

Our Response

IRCS Stage 1 installs 420 brushwood stakes and sediment pockets to rebuild a protective berm within one wet season.

Challenge 2 · Mangrove Decline

Ranger plots recorded a 68% drop in young mangrove survival between 2016 and 2023, thinning the living fence that held sand and stories.

The Cause

Higher base water levels drown root zones for longer than six hours per tide, and hotter pools stress seedlings.

The Impact

Bare gaps expose burial grounds and freshwater wells; crab hunting tracks now sit a metre seaward of the old tree line.

Our Response

Sediment gardens lift plots 30 centimetres and community nurseries deliver 4,500 seedlings with Elders guiding species mixes.

Challenge 3 · Infrastructure Disruption

Power, water, and evacuation tracks take repeat hits, with seven major repair bills logged since 2019.

The Cause

Freight delays leave exposed edges without cover, and concrete deflects wave energy onto roads and the airstrip bund.

The Impact

Emergency funding replaces planned upgrades; the clinic access ramp has been rebuilt three times in five years.

Our Response

IRCS workstreams train 24 local crew members to build, monitor, and maintain modular units using Saibai-sourced brushwood and fill.

Response Gap

Why current approaches fall short

Past works stay rigid, cost heavy, and overlook Saibai's living coastline.

Rigid seawalls

The 2010 concrete wall cost $12 million yet undercut within two wet seasons, forcing constant patching along the school frontage.

Imported fill

Forty-five barge loads of rock were shipped in 2022 at $18k each; half washed off the flat when the first cyclone set hit.

Fragmented projects

Short contracts end before monitoring starts, so Elders and rangers are left without resources to keep the repairs alive.

Hard Defences vs Living Coastlines

Why Not a Seawall?

Traditional seawalls lock Saibai into a costly battle with the sea instead of rebuilding the coastline.

Seawalls lock Saibai into a losing loop

Families remember the 2010 concrete wall: within two wet seasons the footings sank, waves bounced sideways, and new washouts opened beside the school road.

Environmental Cost

  • 2014 ranger counts showed 70% mangrove loss beside the wall.
  • Wave rebound digs half-metre scour pits at the open ends.
  • Trapped salt water sours gardens and turtle nesting flats.
  • No mud or carbon is stored; Country stays bare.

Economic Burden

  • The 2019 repair cost $4.5 million for just 320 m of wall.
  • Each freight barge season adds over $120k before work begins.
  • Patching follows every king tide; budgets never catch up.
  • Outside crews take the spend, leaving few local wages.

Social Disconnect

  • Concrete steps block walking tracks to fishing rocks.
  • Young crews watch while visiting teams make the calls.
  • Approvals arrive after cyclone season has already hit.
  • Walls feel like a fence between people and sea.

What IRCS offers instead

IRCS uses brushwood, mud, and mangroves that Elders already trust. Country heals while Saibai hands guide the work.

  • Local timber and palm make up the modules, so crews build and fix on-island.
  • Sediment cells lift flats 20-40 cm, letting mangroves and turtles return.
  • Ranger patrols log mud and mangrove growth, unlocking blue-carbon income.

Pressure Points

Key forces reshaping Saibai's coast

Process

Mangrove loss

Current impact

Ranger surveys logged 11 hectares of mangrove fringe in 2016; only 7 hectares remained by 2024.

Future risk

Without new roots, foreshore retreat could reach 1 m per year, pushing salt into family gardens and wells.

Process

Erosion & flooding

Current impact

Drone transects show 0.6 m annual retreat, leaving power poles and the airstrip bund exposed each wet season.

Future risk

Continued retreat threatens to cut the evacuation road by 2028, forcing staged moves inland.

Process

Cultural displacement

Current impact

Families spend up to two weeks each wet season on emergency clean-ups, delaying cultural programs and youth ranger training.

Future risk

If the foreshore keeps failing, mainland relocation becomes the only affordable option, eroding intergenerational knowledge on Saibai.

Our Opportunity

See how IRCS restores balance between land, water, and community

The Integrated Regenerative Coastal System links brushwood bund cells, sediment gardens, and ranger-led monitoring. A 300 m pilot line is designed to trap 18 tonnes of mud each spring tide.

Modular units are built from Saibai timber and palm, creating 24 crew roles, blue-carbon reporting income, and a shoreline that gets stronger with every season on Country.
