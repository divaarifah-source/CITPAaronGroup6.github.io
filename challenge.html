Why Saibai Is at Risk

Saibai Island sits within the Torres Strait, a low-lying mud island only kilometres from Papua New Guinea. Climate shifts are reshaping its coastline and livelihood.

Saibai’s foundations are layers of mangrove sediment, resting barely above sea level. Intensifying king tides, storm surges, and sea-level rise now inundate homes several times a year. Saltwater intrusion contaminates precious freshwater lenses, while chronic flooding accelerates shoreline loss and threatens food production and infrastructure.

These pressures compound on a community whose cultural identity is inseparable from Country. Elders describe how burial grounds and culturally significant trees are being swept away, eroding stories that have anchored Saibai life for generations.

What the Crisis Looks Like

Environmental systems, cultural heritage, and economic stability are bound together — each now under strain.

Environmental Impact

The protective mangrove fringe has been dying back as saltwater inundation and sediment imbalance choke roots. Without this buffer, waves strip away banks, exposing homes, roads, and freshwater springs.

Cultural & Human Impact

Country holds ancestral burial grounds, totemic trees, and community gathering spaces. Rapid erosion undermines these places and raises the spectre of forced relocation — a lived reality for previous generations.

Economic Challenge

Hard-engineered seawalls deliver short respite but fail under extreme events, demanding costly repairs. Millions have been spent on emergency works that do little to rebuild natural resilience.

Problem Statement

How can a culturally sensitive and economically viable coastal protection system be developed to mitigate climate-change-driven erosion and safeguard the physical and cultural survival of the Saibai community, without relying on traditional, high-impact infrastructure?

User Need

The Saibai community needs to implement a resilient and sustainable coastal defense system because climate-change-driven erosion is threatening their ancestral homelands, compromising their limited freshwater supplies, and risking forced displacement from their traditional way of life.

What Has Been Tried — And Why It Falls Short

Concrete Seawalls (2015–2021)

Provided short-term flood defence, yet repeated undercutting and cyclone damage demand ongoing capital works. The rigid walls also reflect wave energy, worsening erosion nearby.

Mangrove Replanting Campaigns

Community-led plantings achieved patches of regrowth, but high mortality persists where sediments are not replenished. Without hydrodynamic support structures, young mangroves cannot establish.

Drainage and Channel Works

Improved tidal flushing in targeted zones yet failed to address sediment starvation. Works remain disconnected from broader coastal processes and cultural governance needs.

A Chance to Regenerate

The Integrated Regenerative Coastal System (IRCS) reframes protection as partnership with nature. By combining brushwood structures, sediment nurseries, and guided mangrove regeneration, the system rebuilds natural barriers while respecting cultural stewardship.

Instead of importing heavy infrastructure, IRCS elevates local knowledge and low-tech maintenance so Saibai can restore its own shorelines and livelihoods.
