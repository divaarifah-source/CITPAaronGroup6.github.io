Project Rollout Overview

Implementation of the IRCS begins with local preparation, material collection, and community training before scaling along Saibai’s vulnerable coastline.

Our deployment sequence keeps decision-making on Country: we co-design pilot works with the Community Steering Group (CSG), verify the system with lived knowledge, and expand only after the community is ready to lead.

24-Month Implementation Roadmap

A two-year program progresses from planning and installation through restoration and verification with Saibai leadership at every step.

Phase 1

Foundation

Months 1–6

Planning

Community Engagement

  • Co-design workshops with Saibai elders and youth
  • Traditional knowledge integration sessions
  • Local employment training program launch

Technical Preparation

  • Environmental impact assessment and permits
  • Detailed site surveys and sediment analysis
  • Material procurement (local timber sourcing)
  • Blue Carbon baseline assessment

Phase 2

Installation

Months 7–12

Construction

Bund Deployment

  • Install 2 km of brushwood bunds (pilot section)
  • 50% local labour (15–20 community members employed)
  • Monthly monitoring of sediment accumulation

Sediment Monitoring

  • Install sensor array for water level and sediment load
  • Bi-weekly topographic surveys
  • Partnership with James Cook University for research
  • Community-led observation protocol training

Phase 3

Restoration

Months 13–18

Planting

Mangrove Planting

  • Propagule collection from healthy donor sites
  • 10,000+ seedlings planted in accreted zones
  • Community planting days celebrating cultural significance
  • Initial survival monitoring with a 70%+ target

Ecosystem Development

  • Natural colonisation by native species
  • Fish and crab habitat surveys
  • Bird nesting monitoring
  • Community education on ecosystem services

Phase 4

Validation

Months 19–24

Verification

Performance Assessment

  • Final sediment accretion measurement (target 20 cm+)
  • Mangrove growth and survival evaluation
  • Storm resilience testing against seasonal cyclones
  • Comparison with unprotected control sections

Blue Carbon Certification

  • Verra VCS and CCB Standards verification audit
  • Carbon stock quantification for soil and biomass
  • First carbon credit issuance (estimated 500–800 credits)
  • Revenue distribution plan finalised with community

Community Engagement Strategy

Traditional knowledge and co-design anchor each decision so the IRCS reflects cultural rhythms.

The IRCS is co-designed with Saibai elders. Seasonal calendars inform harvesting windows, and workshops ensure every youth, ranger, and elder can read the shoreline’s response.

Storytelling & Mapping Workshops

Gather tidal narratives, historical erosion points, and cultural sites to align bund placement with community memory.

Skill-Building Sessions

Hands-on brushwood bund assembly, lashings, and sediment testing so locals can independently scale the system.

Local Stewardship Training

Introduce simple monitoring tools — measuring sticks, sediment traps, and photo logs — for monthly reporting.

Risk & Mitigation

Contingencies keep the pilot adaptable when weather, resources, or participation shift.

Risk Impact Mitigation Strategy
Cyclone or High Storm Season Bund damage or loss. Build modular segments for rapid replacement; schedule construction outside peak cyclones.
Material Shortage Delayed construction. Stage harvesting and source brushwood from multiple nearby islands.
Labour or Engagement Drop Slower progress and knowledge gaps. Provide continuous paid work via Blue Carbon funding and rotate responsibilities.
Poor Sediment Retention Reduced shoreline recovery. Adjust bund spacing, porosity, and supplement with sediment nourishment based on monitoring.

Monitoring & Evaluation

Evidence-based feedback loops maintain performance and inform scale-up decisions.

The pilot phase is tracked monthly by the CSG, with quarterly validation by the university team. Data anchors each adaptation cycle and confirms when expansion is safe.

Sediment Height Change

Measured in centimetres per month along fixed transects.

Mangrove Survival

Percentage of propagules alive after each wet and dry season.

Bund Integrity

Structural checks after storms to log repairs or replacements.

Community Participation

Volunteer and paid hours logged to demonstrate stewardship.

Observe

Visual inspections and sensor reads.

Record

Log data in CSG sheets and photo journals.

Adapt

Refine bund density or planting schedule.

Improve

Scale effective practices to new shoreline segments.

Long-Term Vision

The pilot sets the foundation for a Saibai-led “Blue Belt” across the Torres Strait.

The IRCS proves that community-built, nature-based engineering can regenerate coastlines. After pilot success, we expand the model to other islands, pairing brushwood infrastructure with traditional stewardship and blue carbon finance.

Future State

Connected regenerative systems restore ecology, safeguard livelihoods, and finance community care for decades.

Financial Sovereignty Through Blue Carbon

A regenerative revenue model keeps carbon income on Saibai, funding long-term stewardship and community priorities.

Revenue Model

$50–70

Per carbon credit (premium pricing for Indigenous co-benefits and biodiversity value).

8,000–12,000

Credits generated annually at full scale (15–20 hectares of restored mangroves).

$400K–600K

Annual revenue potential after certification and verification costs are covered.

Revenue Allocation

  • Maintenance & Monitoring 20%
  • Community Development Fund 40%
  • Climate Adaptation Reserve 25%
  • System Expansion (New Areas) 15%

Revenue is controlled by the Saibai Community Trust, ensuring local decision-making and intergenerational benefit.

Why This is Financial Sovereignty

Unlike grant-dependent projects, the IRCS activates a perpetual income stream managed on Country. After an 8–10 year payback period, 100% of Blue Carbon revenue remains on Saibai to fund education, health, cultural programs, and future climate adaptation. The community transitions from climate risk to climate enterprise.
